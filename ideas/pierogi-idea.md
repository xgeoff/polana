* **Fresh herbs (basil)**

* Polish *pierogi ruskie* (cheese-based, but heavier)
* Italian ricotta fillings (lighter, herb-forward)

## My honest take on the flavor profile

**What works really well:**

* Garlic + basil is a proven, bright combination
* Cottage cheese keeps it light and slightly tangy
* It’ll feel *fresh*, not heavy like potato fillings
* You’re avoiding the “dense starch bomb” effect of traditional fillings

**Where it might fall short (if left as-is):**

* Cottage cheese alone can be a bit *watery + bland*
* Basil sautéed too long loses its brightness
* You may miss **depth / salt / umami**

---

## How I’d refine your idea (without losing your intent)

If you want this to really land, I’d tweak—not change—your concept:

### 1. Fix the texture

* Drain the cottage cheese (very important)
* Or blend it slightly for a smoother filling

### 2. Adjust the herb approach

Instead of fully sautéing basil:

* Sauté **garlic lightly**
* Add basil at the end or keep some fresh

This keeps it vibrant instead of dull/olive-colored

### 3. Add one “anchor” flavor

Pick one of these to deepen it:

* **Parmesan** (or similar hard cheese) → adds umami + salt
* Lemon zest → bright, fresh contrast
* Black pepper → warmth
* A tiny bit of butter → rounds everything out

### 4. Optional upgrade (highly recommended)

Mix in:

* Ricotta (for creaminess) **or**
* Farmer’s cheese (more Eastern European vibe)

## What it will taste like (if done right)

* Light, slightly tangy
* Herbaceous and fresh
* Garlicky but not heavy
* Creamy without being rich
* Closer to a “spring pierogi” than a traditional one

## One important question

How are you planning to finish them?

Because this filling pairs very differently depending on the finish:

* **Boiled + butter** → classic, but might feel too simple
* **Pan-fried after boiling** → much better (adds contrast)
* **With sour cream** → adds needed richness
* **With a drizzle of olive oil + cracked pepper** → leans into your fresh concept
