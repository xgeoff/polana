I absolutely think sorrel deserves a place on the menu. In fact, I'd argue it's **more quintessentially Eastern European than nasturtium**.

Sorrel (*Rumex acetosa*) grows wild and has been cultivated across Poland, Lithuania, Belarus, Ukraine, and Russia for centuries. Its bright, lemony flavor comes from oxalic acid, which gives it an acidity unlike almost any other leafy green. It's one of those ingredients that instantly evokes spring in much of Eastern Europe.

## Sorrel soup

This is probably the most iconic use. Polish **zupa szczawiowa** ("sorrel soup") is a classic.

It typically includes:

* Homemade chicken or vegetable stock
* Potatoes
* Sorrel
* Carrots and onions
* Dill or parsley
* A dollop of sour cream
* Hard-boiled egg

It's comforting but has a lively tang that keeps it from feeling heavy. It's a perfect spring soup.

For Polana, I could see elevating it just a bit:

* Rich chicken stock
* Baby potatoes
* Fresh sorrel added at the very end
* Soft-poached egg instead of hard-boiled
* Crème fraîche or cultured sour cream
* Fresh dill
* Rustic rye toast

Still recognizably Polish, just plated with a lighter touch.

## Salads

Sorrel is excellent mixed with milder greens because of its citrusy bite.

Imagine:

* Sorrel
* Butter lettuce
* Thin cucumber
* Radishes
* Farmer's cheese
* Dill
* Apple vinaigrette

It would taste incredibly fresh.

## Sautéed

People often overlook sorrel cooked as a vegetable. Like spinach, it wilts dramatically, but the flavor becomes smoother while retaining that pleasant acidity.

Cooked with:

* Butter
* Shallots
* Garlic
* A splash of cream

it's wonderful alongside fish, roast chicken, pork, or kielbasa.

## Sauce

The French are famous for sorrel sauce with salmon, but there's no reason it couldn't work in your bistro.

A pan-seared trout or salmon with a light sorrel cream sauce and roasted potatoes would fit beautifully.

## The menu as it's starting to emerge

One thing I notice is that your restaurant is naturally developing a theme that isn't just "Polish food." It's becoming **the cuisine of the Eastern European garden.**

You're returning again and again to ingredients like:

* Dill
* Sorrel
* Radishes
* Nasturtium
* Farmer's cheese
* Apples
* Rye
* Buckwheat
* Mushrooms
* Cabbage
* Beets
* Sunflower seeds
* Fresh herbs

Those ingredients are humble individually, but in combination they create a very distinctive identity. They also feel fresh and modern, which differentiates Polana from restaurants that lean heavily on hearty winter dishes.

One other herb I'd encourage you to explore is **lovage**. It has a celery-like aroma with a deeper, almost savory quality and is used in Poland and neighboring countries in soups, broths, and potato dishes. It isn't common in American restaurants, but it's easy to grow and could become another signature ingredient that reinforces Polana's "garden" identity.

I can easily imagine guests leaving Polana saying, *"Everything tasted so fresh. I didn't realize Eastern European food could taste like that."* That would be a powerful niche to occupy.
