Lovage is one of those ingredients that makes chefs light up because it has such a distinctive personality. If dill is the bright, cheerful herb of Eastern Europe, **lovage is its deep, aromatic cousin**.

It tastes somewhere between:

* Celery leaves
* Parsley
* A little anise
* A rich herbal broth

Not licorice-forward—just incredibly savory. A few leaves can transform a soup.

One thing I've noticed over all our menu brainstorming is that you're **not trying to reinvent Polish cuisine**. You're trying to uncover a version that Americans haven't really experienced.

A lot of Polish restaurants in the U.S. emphasize the hearty, celebratory dishes—pierogi, kielbasa, cabbage rolls, schnitzel, and big portions of potatoes. Those are wonderful, but they're only part of the story.

You're gravitating toward the cuisine of:

* the cottage garden,
* the forest edge,
* the orchard,
* the meadow.

That's where the name **Polana** ("forest glade" or "clearing") becomes more than just a pretty word. It starts to describe the entire culinary philosophy: fresh herbs, wildflowers, orchard fruit, mushrooms, rye, cultured dairy, and simple, seasonal cooking.

I honestly think that's a much more compelling identity than simply being "another Polish restaurant." It's the kind of place where someone orders a salad because it's *the* salad, not because they feel like they should eat something healthy before the pierogi.
