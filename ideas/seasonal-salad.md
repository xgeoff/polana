I think you're identifying exactly why this idea has legs. It doesn't feel *invented*—it feels like something that *could have been* a traditional country salad, even if it isn't.

One interesting historical note is that **nasturtiums are not native to Eastern Europe**. They originated in South America (primarily Peru and Bolivia) and were brought to Europe in the 1500s. By the 17th and 18th centuries they had spread widely as ornamental and kitchen garden plants. Today they're grown throughout Europe, including Poland, Ukraine, the Czech Republic, and the Baltics, but they're not deeply rooted in traditional Polish cuisine the way dill, sorrel, or horseradish are.

That actually works in your favor.

Your concept isn't trying to recreate a peasant dish from 1850. It's **contemporary Polish**—using ingredients that grow well in a Polish garden and pairing them in a way that feels authentic to the region.

The flavor profile is very Eastern European:

* Peppery greens
* Radishes
* Dill
* Farmer's cheese (twaróg)
* Sunflower seeds
* Apples or honey
* Apple cider vinegar

Those are all flavors people associate with Poland and its neighbors.

From an American perspective, I think it also hits a sweet spot. Most diners have had:

* arugula
* watercress
* radishes
* goat cheese
* sunflower seeds

Nasturtium is the only unfamiliar ingredient, but it tastes familiar enough that people won't find it strange. If you simply describe it as "peppery garden greens," most guests will understand what they're getting.

### One thing I would lean into

Rather than calling it a "nasturtium salad," I'd make the story about **the garden**.

For example:

**Polana Garden Salad**

> Nasturtium and tender radish greens, breakfast radishes, cucumber ribbons, farmer's cheese, toasted sunflower seeds, dill, and honey-apple vinaigrette.

The guest doesn't have to know what nasturtium is before ordering it. They discover it naturally.

### A seasonal evolution

I also love that this salad could change with the seasons:

* **Spring:** nasturtium, radish greens, peas, dill
* **Summer:** add tomatoes and cucumbers from the garden
* **Late summer:** more nasturtium flowers and herbs
* **Autumn:** sliced apples, toasted walnuts or hazelnuts, perhaps roasted beets

That kind of seasonal variation fits perfectly with the identity you've been developing for **Polana**. It feels like the sort of salad a small bistro with its own kitchen garden would serve—distinctive enough that guests remember it, but approachable enough that they'll order it again.
